What is Regression?

Regression is a technique of predicting an output, or dependent variable, given one or more inputs, or independent variables.

Mathematically, let XXX be the inputs and yyy be the outputs. Then, the regression equation can be shown as E(y∣X)=f(X).\mathbb{E}(y|X) = f(X).E(y∣X)=f(X).

Taking the expectation of the outputs demonstrates a minimization of the mean squared error (MSE): the squared difference between the data points and the estimator function. By minimizing this, we can reasonably be sure that we have a good estimator function, based on the data we have.

Linear regression specifies the use of a straight line for the regression, rather than a curve.

So, let X={x1,x2,...,xn}X=\{x_1,x_2,...,x_n\}X={x1​,x2​,...,xn​} be the inputs, $ B ={\beta_1,\beta_2,…,\beta_n}$ be the coefficients, and yyy be the outputs. Then the linear regression model is y=X⋅B=x1⋅β1+x2⋅β2+...+xn⋅βn.y=X \cdot B=x_1 \cdot \beta_1 + x_2 \cdot \beta_2 + ... + x_n \cdot \beta_n.y=X⋅B=x1​⋅β1​+x2​⋅β2​+...+xn​⋅βn​.

The only problem with this equation is the noise. It is rare that the inputs and outputs are perfectly correlated, so it is safe to assume there is some noise variable for each point, randomly distributed on the Gaussian curve, leaving us with the equation y=X⋅B+σϵy=X \cdot B + \sigma \epsilony=X⋅B+σϵ

To solve for the coefficients, there are several matrix manipulations we must make to preserve the shape of the input features.

First, we multiply by the transpose of XXX to make XTXX^TXXTX a square, then multiply by the inverse of XTXX^TXXTX (assuming XTXX^TXXTX is invertible).

$$\begin{align} X^Ty &= X^TX \cdot B + \sigma X^T \epsilon \

(XTX){-1}(X^Ty) &= (XTX){-1}(X^TX) \cdot B + \sigma (XTX){-1}(X^T \epsilon) \end{align}$$

Since a matrix times its own inverse is equal to the identity matrix, we then solve for the coefficient vector, BBB.

$$\begin{align}

(XTX){-1}(X^Ty) &= B + \sigma (XTX){-1}(X^T \epsilon) \

B &= (XTX){-1}(X^Ty) - \sigma (XTX){-1}(X^T \epsilon) \

\end{align}$$

The expectation (mean) of the noise variable is zero, so taking the expectation of the coefficients (evaluating MSE) eliminates the noise variable, leaving us with the expected value of the coefficients.

Bˉ=(XTX)−1(XTy)\bar{B} = (X^TX)^{-1}(X^Ty)Bˉ=(XTX)−1(XTy)

If we insert these estimated coefficients back into the equation, we get our predicted outputs:

y^=X⋅(XTX)−1(XTy)\hat{y} = X \cdot (X^TX)^{-1}(X^Ty)y^​=X⋅(XTX)−1(XTy)

The last important piece of this puzzle, is the weights. Certain features are going to correlate more with the output and the weights are how correlated features are emphasized and uncorrelated or less-correlated features are deemphasized in the estimation process. The weights (WWW) are associated with the inputs, so our final equation looks like this:
